Medical Research

Axolotl genome sequencing (32Gb) completed in 2018 revealed key regeneration genes. Researchers identified PAX3 gene absence, suggesting alternative pathways for tissue regeneration. Source: PacBi

Their cancer resistance is being studied - axolotls have a staggeringly low rate of cancer compared to other mammals despite frequent cell division during regeneration. Source: San Diego Zoo

Regeneration Studies

Blastema cells (regeneration cells) can regenerate limbs 100+ times without loss of function. Researchers are studying their epigenetic regulation to understand regeneration limits. Source: Wikipedia - Regeneration

Recent breakthroughs include successful transplantation of axolotl stem cells into mice, showing potential for cross-species medical applications. Source: Cell Reports

"Understanding axolotl regeneration could unlock human regenerative medicine within our lifetime."
